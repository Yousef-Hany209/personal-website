STEM Journal Evaluation Rubric

Criteria Excellent (Blue) Good Needs Improvement
Personal Reflection Deep insight with clear reasoning and personal examples.
Example: "When I first started my project, I faced difficulties in wiring my circuit. However, after troubleshooting, I realized my mistake and learned the importance of proper insulation."		 Reflects on the topic with some personal connection but lacks deep insight.
Example: "I found the project challenging but rewarding."		 Minimal or no personal reflection. Lacks clear reasoning.
Example: "The project was good."
Engineering Design Process (EDP) Clearly explains and applies EDP steps with strong technical details and real project applications.
Example: "In the 'Define the Problem' phase, we identified energy loss in our system. During 'Testing,' we experimented with different wire gauges to reduce resistance."		 Mentions EDP steps but with a general or surface-level connection to the project.
Example: "We used the Engineering Design Process to improve our design."		 Weak or no mention of EDP; lacks clear connection to the project.
Example: "We made changes to our project based on feedback."
Connections to STEM Subjects Effectively links STEM subjects (Physics, Math, Chemistry, Biology, CS) to the project with detailed explanations and examples.
Example: "Using Ohm's Law (V = IR), we calculated the optimal resistance for our circuit, ensuring maximum efficiency."		 Makes some connections but lacks depth or specificity.
Example: "Physics principles helped us understand electricity."		 Weak or no connection to STEM subjects. Explanations are unclear or missing.
Example: "We used some science in our project."
Problem-Solving and Justification Strong justification for decisions using data, calculations, or logical reasoning. Demonstrates critical thinking.
Example: "Our wind turbine design was chosen based on aerodynamic principles. We tested three different blade angles and found 30Â° to be the most efficient, generating 0.05 kWh of energy."		 Justifies decisions but lacks strong supporting details or evidence.
Example: "We adjusted the blade angle to improve efficiency."		 Weak or no justification. No supporting data or reasoning.
Example: "We changed the design to make it better."
Communication and Clarity Well-structured response with smooth transitions; uses appropriate formal, scientific language.
Example: "The data from our experiments clearly demonstrate a direct correlation between blade angle and energy output, confirming our hypothesis."		 Mostly clear, but may have minor organizational or clarity issues.
Example: "We tested different blade angles and recorded the results."		 Poor organization or unclear writing. Difficult to follow.
Example: "We did an experiment. It was good."
